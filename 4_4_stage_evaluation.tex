We evaluated the efficiency of the three stages in $\ourtool$: $\sample$, $\extract$, and $\generalize$. Specifically, we compared $\ourtool$ against variants that skip each stage in Algorithm~2. To assess the $\sample$ stage, which reduces the inefficiency of generating redundant test cases, we defined a variant that uses the solver-based assignment ($\assignments$) for satisfiable queries without sampling at line~7. In the $\extract$ variant, we omitted line~11 and stored the original path conditions ($\pc$) of unsatisfiable queries, instead of extracting their core constraints. Lastly, for the $\generalize$ stage, the corresponding variant bypasses line~14 and stores the query data refined by the $\sample$ and $\extract$ stages without transforming it into the generalized representation ($\pc'$, $\assignments'$).

Table~\ref{tab:stage_evaluation} demonstrates that all stages of $\ourtool$ contribute to covering more branches and improving bug-finding ability. $\ourtool$ achieved 19.7\%, 7.8\%, and 5.8\% higher branch coverage than the variants without the $\sample$, $\extract$, and $\generalize$ stages, respectively. The release-wise results also show consistent improvements: $\ourtool$ outperforms the variants by 10.9\% on average for the second release and by 11.2\% for the third release. In terms of bug finding, $\ourtool$ detected five times more bugs than the $\sample$-skipping variant and 25\% more bugs than the $\extract$- and $\generalize$-skipping variants.

The performance advantage of $\ourtool$ over the variants stems from each stage effectively addressing its objective. By transforming assignments of satisfiable queries, the $\sample$ stage reduced the redundant-test-case ratio from 90.5\% to 41.7\% for the second release and from 94.1\% to 47.3\% for the third release. The $\extract$ stage reduced storage cost by 15.4\% and matched 16.7\% more path conditions compared with the variant. In particular, unlike the other stages, the $\extract$ stage incurs substantial overhead for core extraction, consuming 7.4\% of the total budget (0.9h/12h), yet still outperforms the corresponding variant. Through the $\generalize$ stage, $\ourtool$ reduced storage cost by 15.1\% and matched 15.2\% more path conditions in subsequent-release testing.

\myparagraph{\bf Efficiency of matching method}
We also investigated the efficiency of the matching method described in Section~\ref{sec:compare}. During subsequent-release testing, this method prioritizes refined query data based on similarity and determines whether it matches explored path conditions using feasible-range containment. We compared this method against a variant that compares query data in an arbitrary order and reports a match when two path conditions are exactly identical.

As a result, our method outperformed the variant in both branch coverage and bug detection when testing the two subsequent releases. On average, $\ourtool$ covered 17.9\% more branches than the variant and detected five bugs, three more than the variant. In practice, similarity-based prioritization allowed $\ourtool$ to perform only 2.2 comparisons on average per matched path condition, which is 63.2\% fewer than the variant.
