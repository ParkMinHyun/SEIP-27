\subsection{The Draft Sequence on the Capture Timeout Path}
\label{sec:draft-background}

A shutter event requests multiple image buffers and their metadata.
As Figure~\ref{fig:draft-pipeline} shows, the camera framework uses these inputs for two complementary purposes.
The post-processing pipeline performs multi-frame compositing, applies single-frame processing to the composited image, and stores the result as the final image.
In parallel, the Draft Sequence selects and stores a representative frame as the Draft image for early display to the user.
Upon successful completion of post-processing, the final image replaces the Draft image; if post-processing fails or is interrupted, the Draft image remains available for recovery.
The Draft Sequence is therefore both user-facing and reliability-critical.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Increasing Draft workload.}
As post-processing became more compute- and memory-intensive across product generations, the framework began deferring post-processing for selected shot modes until the camera application entered the background to preserve foreground responsiveness.
This deferral kept the Draft image visible for longer, increasing the importance of visual consistency with the final image.
The Draft Sequence consequently incorporated lightweight single-frame processing to reduce conspicuous differences from the final image.
Most recently, we have investigated extending the Draft Sequence with a lightweight counterpart to the multi-frame compositing stage.
These additions narrow the visual gap between the Draft image and the final image but increase the workload on the Capture Timeout path.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Ordered single-worker execution.}
The framework executes Draft Sequences on a single worker thread in capture order.
This deliberate production design bounds active Draft processing to one sequence.
Concurrent execution would require buffering out-of-order results and would overlap image processing workloads across sequences, causing peak compute and memory demand to scale with the number of active sequences.
