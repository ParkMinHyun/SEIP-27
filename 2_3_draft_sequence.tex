\subsection{The Draft Sequence on the Capture Timeout Path}
\label{sec:draft-background}

A shutter event requests multiple image buffers and their metadata.
As Figure~\ref{fig:draft-pipeline} shows, the camera framework uses these inputs for two complementary purposes.
The post-processing pipeline performs multi-frame compositing, applies single-frame processing to the composited image, and stores the result as the final photograph.
In parallel, the Draft Sequence selects and stores a representative frame for early display to the user.
Upon successful completion of post-processing, the final photograph replaces this frame; if post-processing fails or is interrupted, the stored frame remains available for recovery.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Increasing Draft workload.}
As post-processing became more compute- and memory-intensive across product generations, the framework began deferring final composition for selected shot modes until the camera application entered the background to preserve foreground responsiveness.
This deferral kept the Draft image visible for longer, increasing the importance of visual consistency with the final photograph.
This need drove a three-stage evolution of the Draft Sequence.
Initially, it applied no image effects.
It then incorporated lightweight single-frame processing to reduce conspicuous differences from the final photograph.
Most recently, the Draft Sequence was extended with a lightweight counterpart to the multi-frame compositing stage in the post-processing pipeline.
These additions reduce visual differences between the Draft image and the final photograph but add work directly to the Capture Timeout path.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Ordered single-worker execution.}
The framework executes Draft Sequences on a single worker thread in capture order.
This deliberate production design bounds active Draft processing to one sequence.
Running multiple Draft Sequences concurrently would require buffering out-of-order results and would make their workloads compete with preview and capture for compute and memory resources.
Under parallel capture, however, requests can be admitted faster than the worker completes their Draft Sequences.
Consequently, parallel capture shortens the shot-to-shot interval at the cost of Draft queueing: later captures spend part of their timeout budgets waiting behind earlier Draft Sequences.
