\section{Industrial Context and Problem Formulation}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Draft Delivery on the Capture-Critical Path}
\label{sec:draft-background}

A shutter event produces multiple image buffers. The full post-processing path
collects the required buffers, performs multi-frame composition and image
enhancement, encodes the result, and stores the final JPEG. As these operations
have become more compute- and memory-intensive, selected capture modes defer
full composition until the camera application enters the background, thereby
avoiding contention with preview rendering and preparation for the next shot.

Deferral creates a delivery gap, which the framework bridges with the Draft
Sequence. The Draft path selects its input before full composition completes,
performs a bounded amount of processing, writes a JPEG and its media-database
entry, and retains the image as a recovery result. The gallery thumbnail can
therefore expose the capture early; when full post-processing finishes, the
framework replaces the Draft with the final image. The Draft is consequently
both a user-visible intermediate result and a reliability mechanism, not an
internal preview artifact.

The growing difference between the two paths motivated richer Draft
processing. The original Draft applied no image effect, and later versions
added single-frame Filter and Watermark stages. Portrait mode raised the next
requirement. Its final result applies multi-frame processing and Bokeh, and its
full composition is sufficiently expensive to benefit from background
deferral. An untreated Draft, however, can look qualitatively different from
the final portrait. Lightweight multi-frame Draft Bokeh was developed to
provide an approximate early portrait while preserving background execution
for the heavier final composition. Figure~\ref{fig:draft-pipeline} summarizes
this intended transition.

\subsection{Parallel Capture Creates Cross-Shot Interference}
\label{sec:timeout}

% FACT CHECK: confirm the 7-second product deadline before submission.
The framework raises a \emph{Capture Timeout} when the Draft result is not
delivered within the fixed 7-second deadline \deadline. Parallel capture
changes how this deadline is consumed. Once the HAL is ready to accept another
request, it can report capture availability before all buffers from the
preceding shot have arrived and before that shot's Draft Sequence has
finished. The framework can then notify the camera application, which may
start the next capture immediately. Draft Sequences, however, share a single
worker and therefore execute in order.

Figure~\ref{fig:overlap} illustrates the resulting interference. Capture $i$
starts its Draft as soon as its input is ready. Capture $i{+}1$ begins while
that Draft is still running and must wait for the same worker; capture $i{+}2$
waits behind both predecessors. Crucially, each deadline starts at its own
capture event rather than at the time its Draft obtains the worker. Queueing
therefore consumes a later capture's budget before any of its Draft stages can
run. Even with identical stage durations, the remaining budget shrinks across
the burst until a mandatory stage crosses the deadline.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{\input{figures/fig_draft_overlap}}
  \caption{Cross-shot deadline interference under parallel capture. Draft
  Sequences queue on one worker, but each deadline starts at the corresponding
  capture. The hatched waiting interval therefore consumes progressively more
  budget, until capture $i{+}2$ times out during mandatory encoding.}
  \label{fig:overlap}
\end{figure}

This behavior makes tail latency more important than average operation time.
A slowdown caused by thermal throttling, memory pressure, scheduling, or I/O
can lengthen one Draft Sequence and transfer its delay to every capture behind
it. The relevant unit of risk is therefore the \emph{queued and remaining
sequence}, not Bokeh or Filter measured in isolation.

\subsection{A Thermal Threshold Cannot Separate Safe and Unsafe Captures}
\label{sec:guard-limit}

Parallel capture predated the current thermal safeguard. The safeguard was
introduced later, after a Filter-related Capture Timeout on a lower-tier
model at thermal level~4. Because the camera framework shares the policy across
models, the resulting rule disables optional Draft stages at level~4 and above
on flagship devices as well. This static rule is appropriate for containing a
known release incident, but it neither observes the active workload nor the
queue that determines the remaining deadline.

Table~\ref{tab:timeout_index} characterizes the limitation on a
current-generation flagship. For this experiment, we bypassed the deployed
guard and forced either Bokeh alone or Bokeh with Filter throughout 30-shot
portrait bursts. We varied thermal level, output resolution, and memory
pressure, and recorded the first capture that timed out.

The boundary fails in both directions. It is \emph{under-protective} below
level~4: Bokeh with Filter first times out at capture 29 under normal 24MP
capture even at level~0, and at capture 24 under normal 12MP capture at
level~2. Memory pressure moves failures earlier; for example, at level~3 the
first 24MP Bokeh-plus-Filter timeout moves from capture 12 to capture 5. It is
also \emph{over-restrictive} at and above the boundary. At level~4, the normal
12MP traces complete the first nine captures with Bokeh and Filter and the
first 15 with Bokeh alone, yet the deployed rule disables those stages from
the first capture.

These measurements do not imply that the early captures at level~4 are always
safe. Rather, they show that thermal level is not a sufficient decision
variable: the outcome at one level changes with resolution, memory pressure,
selected stages, and the backlog inherited from previous shots. Thermal state
is useful context for estimating execution time, but no nontrivial scalar
threshold in Table~\ref{tab:timeout_index} separates safe and unsafe captures.

\subsection{Problem Formulation and Design Requirements}
\label{sec:control-problem}

Let capture $i$ start at time $a_i$, let its Draft Sequence obtain the worker
at time $s_i$, and let \deadline{} be the fixed deadline measured from
$a_i$. The budget remaining when Draft execution starts is

\begin{equation}
  \budget_i = \deadline - (s_i-a_i).
  \label{eq:remaining-budget}
\end{equation}

Let $\admitted_i$ denote the optional stages selected for capture $i$, and let
$\dur_i(\admitted_i)$ be the execution time from Draft start through delivery,
including the required stages and mandatory tail. The capture is deadline-safe
only if

\begin{equation}
  \dur_i(\admitted_i) \leq \budget_i.
  \label{eq:draft-deadline}
\end{equation}

Equation~\ref{eq:draft-deadline} exposes two different control needs.
Admission can reduce $\dur_i(\admitted_i)$ by omitting Bokeh or Filter for
the current capture, but it cannot restore budget already consumed before
$s_i$. Furthermore, if captures arrive faster than the single worker can
complete them, repeated admission decisions alone cannot stop $\budget_i$
from collapsing across the burst. The framework must also control when the next
capture may begin. Delaying the application-facing \texttt{captureAvailable}
callback does not extend the current capture's deadline; it starts the next
capture later, after queued Draft work has progressed, and thereby preserves
more budget for that future capture.

The industrial objective is consequently lexicographic: (1) prevent every
Capture Timeout, because deadline misses are release blocking; (2) retain the
preferred Draft effects on as many captures as the runtime state safely
allows; and (3) minimize added callback delay and its effect on shot-to-shot
responsiveness. Section~\ref{sec:approach} presents the shared tail model and
the coordinated admission and pacing decisions that address these
requirements.
