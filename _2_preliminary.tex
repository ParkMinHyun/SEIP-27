\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:preliminaries}

\subsection{Multi-Frame Capture and the Draft Sequence}\label{sec:draft}

Modern smartphone cameras fuse multiple frames into a single result. In the
commercial Android camera framework studied in this paper, which a major
smartphone vendor deploys as common code across its entire device fleet, a
shutter press makes the hardware abstraction layer (HAL) deliver a burst of
frames that a post-processing pipeline fuses, encodes, and saves as the one
image the user keeps. This pipeline has grown steadily heavier as AI-based
imaging solutions are adopted; for the heaviest shot modes, fusion is even
deferred until the camera application moves to the background, so the final
image may arrive long after the shutter press.

\myparagraph{Draft sequence}
The \emph{draft sequence} hides this delay. Immediately after capture, one
frame of the burst is run through a short ordered chain of processing
stages, written to a file, and inserted into the media database, which is
updated once final post-processing completes. The resulting \emph{draft
image} is effectively the first capture result the user sees: it appears in
the gallery right away and serves as a recovery image if final processing
fails. Originally an unprocessed frame, the draft came to include basic
processing as the visual gap to the final image widened. In portrait mode,
the target of this work, the draft path may run several enhancement
stages---for example, a depth-of-field (bokeh) stage, a dynamic effect, a
stylization filter, and watermarking---followed by a mandatory
\emph{encoding} stage that stores the draft image.

\myparagraph{Workloads and sequences}
We model a draft sequence as an ordered chain of stages, each realizing a
\emph{workload} $\wl \in \wset$ identified by its operation type and the
resolution tier of the frame (plus the output format for encoding), so that
timing samples for ``the same work'' accumulate together across captures.
Workloads play three roles: \emph{admission-gated} workloads (e.g., bokeh
rendering, stylization filters) are expensive optional enhancements that
may be skipped; \emph{observed} workloads (e.g., dynamic effects,
watermarking) always run but must still be accounted for; and the
\emph{mandatory} encoding workload forms the tail $\tail$ of every sequence. At any stage, the work that remains is
the suffix $\wseq = \langle \wl_1, \dots, \wl_m\rangle$ with actual
execution time $\dur(\wseq) = \sum_{j}\dur(\wl_j)$, which varies from
capture to capture with device state, so the pipeline cannot rely on a
static cost table.

\subsection{Capture Timeout in the Draft Sequence}\label{sec:timeout}

\emph{Capture Timeout} is the failure in which a capture request, including
draft-image delivery, does not complete within a hard, framework-enforced
deadline $\deadline$. It surfaces as a shooting failure,
a frozen or crashing camera, or missing capture results, and it is treated
as a release criterion rather than a performance metric: a single
reproducible timeout blocks product release, and a feature that cannot meet
the deadline is dropped or restricted. Because the draft sequence lies
inside the measured interval, the quantity that governs every decision is
the \emph{remaining budget} $\bgt = \deadline - t_{\text{elapsed}}$.

\myparagraph{Why the draft sequence times out}
Two developments turned the draft sequence into a timeout hotspot. First,
\emph{parallel capture}: the next capture, once serialized behind the
previous one, is now accepted as soon as the HAL is ready. Draft sequences,
however, must execute on a single thread, so they queue behind one another,
and a long-running draft sequence makes the next one start with part of its
budget already consumed (Figure~\ref{fig:overlap}); timeout risk thus
depends on recent capture history, not only on a capture's own workloads.
Second, the remaining failures are tail events rather than average-case
inefficiencies: with the obvious optimizations already applied, the
timeouts that remain come from state-dependent factors---thermal
throttling, frequency scaling, scheduling delay, memory pressure and
garbage collection, storage and database latency---under which a normally
fast stage occasionally spikes. What matters is not whether each stage is
fast on average, but whether the whole remaining sequence finishes before
the deadline in unfavorable device states.

\myparagraph{Measured timeout behavior}
Table~\ref{tab:timeout_index} quantifies both effects: we ran 30-shot
continuous portrait captures on a current-generation flagship device,
sweeping its thermal range and enabling the two most expensive draft
stages, bokeh and filter, together, individually, and not at all, with and
without induced memory pressure; each entry is the first shot index at
which Capture Timeout occurred. Timeouts are cumulative: no configuration
fails on the first shot, and failures move earlier as the device
heats---from shot~29 at level~1 down to shot~7 at level~5 with both
stages---exactly the budget erosion of Figure~\ref{fig:overlap}. Risk is
configuration-dependent: at level~3 (Moderate), skipping only bokeh moves
the first timeout from shot~13 to shot~28, and filter-only never times out
under normal conditions. And thermal level alone does not determine risk:
under memory pressure the same levels fail far earlier, and even
filter-only begins to fail at severe levels.

Meanwhile, the pressure to enrich the draft sequence keeps growing. As the
draft--final gap widened, lightweight multi-frame composition in the draft
path became a product requirement---yet even with deliberately light
imaging solutions it could not ship across multiple product generations,
because timeout regressions kept recurring. This experience
motivates the question this paper studies: how can optional draft-stage
processing be admitted aggressively when it is safe, without ever
committing the sequence to work it cannot finish in time?

\subsection{Naive Approaches}\label{sec:naive}

\myparagraph{Deployed practice: thermal-level guards}
The defense accumulated in practice is a set of scattered guards keyed on
device state, most prominently the thermal level:

\noindent
\begin{minipage}{\columnwidth}
\begin{lstlisting}[numbers=none]
if (thermalLevel >= THERMAL_GUARD_LEVEL) {
    return  // skip draft-stage processing
}
\end{lstlisting}
\end{minipage}

\noindent
Introduced after timeouts were found to cluster above a thermal threshold
and later tightened when failures appeared on a lower-tier device, the
guard applies fleet-wide because the framework ships as common code without
per-model branching. Table~\ref{tab:timeout_index} shows why this is
inadequate on both sides: with both stages enabled, timeouts already occur
at the lower thermal levels, below the guard threshold (false negatives),
while filter-only never times out even at the critical level, yet the guard
skips \emph{all} draft-stage processing once the threshold is reached (false
positives).
Both errors are user-visible: a skipped effect that later appears in the
final image yields a draft--final inconsistency, and independent per-shot
decisions cause shot-to-shot jitter in continuous capture.

\myparagraph{Simple predictors}
Replacing the guard with a straightforward latency predictor does not fare
better. A statically profiled worst-case reservation is safe but assumes a
throttled device on every capture, collapsing the admission rate exactly
when the budget is ample. A running mean or last observed value tracks the
center of the latency distribution rather than its tail, and a fixed
percentage margin cannot repair it, because the right margin is precisely
what differs across devices and thermal states. And deciding each stage in
isolation ignores the structure of the pipeline: admitting a stage because
it alone fits the budget can still starve the mandatory encoding tail
behind it.

\smallskip
\noindent
These observations motivate treating Capture Timeout prevention as an
\emph{admission} problem over the ordered remaining workload sequence:
before an optional stage runs, it is admitted only if a conservative upper
bound on the remaining sequence---the stage plus all work after it,
including the mandatory tail $\tail$---satisfies $\ub(\wseq) \le \bgt$.
The bound must be learned online and on-device from observed durations,
produce usable decisions from few or no samples, track drift as the device
heats and throttles, and remain consistent across sequences that share
workloads. The next section presents our context-aware admission controller
for the draft sequence, built around these requirements.
