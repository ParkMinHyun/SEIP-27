\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:preliminaries}

\subsection{Multi-Frame Capture}\label{sec:multi-frame}

Samsung Galaxy Camera uses multi-frame capture to improve the quality of
the final image. Rather than producing the result from a single sensor
frame, the camera framework expands the selected shooting scenario into a
burst of capture requests that specify the required streams, controls, and
frame sequence. The hardware abstraction layer (HAL) executes this burst on
the camera pipeline and asynchronously returns the captured image buffers
and metadata. These frames are then inserted into the post-processing
pipeline, which performs scenario-specific processing, multi-frame fusion,
encoding, and saving to produce the final image.

As Galaxy camera models have evolved, the post-processing pipeline has incorporated 
an increasing number of AI-based imaging solutions. These
solutions improve capture quality, but they also increase memory pressure
and consume substantial compute resources. Running such heavy processing
immediately after capture can degrade the foreground camera experience, for
example by causing preview drops or reducing UI responsiveness. Therefore,
some expensive shot modes defer full fusion and post-processing until the
camera application switches to the background. This design protects the
interactive shooting experience, but it also means that the final processed
image may be generated long after the shutter event.

\subsection{Draft Sequence}\label{sec:draft}

The \emph{draft sequence} reduces this user-visible delay by decoupling
early image delivery from completion of full post-processing. After capture,
one frame from the multi-frame burst can be saved as a \emph{draft image}
and inserted into the media database before final post-processing finishes;
the database entry is updated when the final processed result becomes
available. The user can therefore check the captured photo quickly without
waiting for full post-processing to complete. The draft image can also serve
as a recovery image if post-processing fails.

The draft sequence has evolved in stages. The first generation performed no
image processing in the draft sequence; it saved an unprocessed frame so
that the capture could be exposed quickly. This became visually awkward for
features whose effects appear immediately in the user interface, such as
filters and watermarks. A second generation therefore applied these
single-frame effects inside the draft sequence, reducing obvious
draft--final mismatches without running the full post-processing pipeline.
As post-processing has continued to evolve, however, the gap has become too
large for single-frame effects alone. A third-generation draft sequence was
therefore implemented to support lightweight multi-frame composition,
starting with portrait shooting, the shot mode in which a single-frame
draft diverges most from the final fused result. Yet this design could not be
enabled in production across two successive product generations because the
additional draft-stage computation repeatedly violated the Capture Timeout
constraint described next.

\subsection{Capture Timeout in the Draft Sequence}\label{sec:timeout}

\emph{Capture Timeout} is the failure in which a capture request, including
draft-image delivery, does not complete within a hard, framework-enforced
deadline $\deadline$. It surfaces as a shooting failure, a frozen or
crashing camera, or missing capture results, and it is treated as a release
criterion rather than a performance metric: even a single reproducible
timeout can block product release, and a feature that cannot meet the
deadline may be dropped or restricted. Because the draft sequence lies
inside the measured interval, the quantity that governs every decision is
the \emph{remaining budget} $\bgt = \deadline - t_{\text{elapsed}}$.

\myparagraph{Why the draft sequence times out}
Two developments turned the draft sequence into a timeout hotspot. First,
parallel capture changed when the next shot can begin. Before parallel
capture, the next capture was effectively serialized until the previous
capture had collected its frames and completed the draft sequence. On
parallel-capture devices, the next capture can be accepted once the HAL is
ready for a new request, even if the previous draft sequence has not
completed. Draft sequences still execute on a single thread, so a new draft
sequence can queue behind an older one and start with part of its timeout
budget already consumed (Figure~\ref{fig:overlap}). Timeout risk therefore
depends on recent capture history, not only on the current capture's own
workloads.

Second, the remaining failures are tail events rather than average-case
inefficiencies. Many obvious optimizations can already be applied in a
production pipeline, including avoiding duplicated work, simplifying logic,
or deferring expensive processing. The failures that remain are often caused
by state-dependent factors---thermal throttling, frequency scaling,
scheduling delay, memory pressure and garbage collection, storage and
database latency---under which a normally fast stage occasionally spikes.
What matters is not whether each stage is fast on average, but whether the
whole remaining sequence finishes before the deadline in unfavorable device
states.

\myparagraph{Measured timeout behavior}
Table~\ref{tab:timeout_index} illustrates this behavior in a 30-shot
continuous portrait burst on a current-generation flagship device. We swept
the overheat level, image-size tier, memory condition, and the two
expensive draft-stage configurations that matter most for portrait
admission, bokeh with filter and bokeh alone; each entry reports the first
shot index at which Capture Timeout occurred. Timeouts are cumulative: no
configuration fails on the first shot, but failures move earlier as the
device heats and the queued draft work erodes the remaining budget. With
bokeh and filter enabled, the 12MP normal case first fails at level~2 on
shot~24 and reaches shot~7 by level~6; the 24MP case is already fragile at
level~0, timing out on shot~29 under normal capture and shot~26 under memory
pressure. Memory pressure shifts the same workload much closer to the
deadline: 24MP bokeh with filter fails by shot~5 at level~3 and by shot~4
at level~4. At level~5 and above, the camera no longer executes the draft
path in 24MP mode and instead falls back to 12MP mode; the 24MP columns
therefore repeat the corresponding 12MP-mode measurements rather than
representing independent 24MP measurements. Risk is also
configuration-dependent. Bokeh alone
usually survives longer than bokeh with filter, but it still times out under
higher heat or pressure, showing that the admission decision must reason
about the remaining sequence, image-size tier, and runtime state together.

Meanwhile, the pressure to enrich the draft sequence keeps growing. As the
draft--final gap widened, lightweight multi-frame composition in the draft
path became a product requirement. Yet even deliberately light imaging
solutions could not be enabled across multiple product generations because
timeout regressions kept recurring. This experience motivates the question
this paper studies: how can optional draft-stage processing be admitted
aggressively when it is safe, without ever committing the sequence to work
it cannot finish in time?

\subsection{Naive Approaches}\label{sec:naive}

\myparagraph{Deployed practice: thermal-level guards}
The defense accumulated in practice is a set of scattered guards keyed on
device state, most prominently the thermal level:

\noindent
\begin{minipage}{\columnwidth}
\begin{lstlisting}[numbers=none]
if (thermalLevel >= THERMAL_GUARD_LEVEL) {
    return  // skip optional work
}
\end{lstlisting}
\end{minipage}

\noindent
Introduced after timeouts were found to cluster above a thermal threshold
and later tightened when failures appeared on a lower-tier device, the
guard applies fleet-wide because the framework ships as common code without
per-model branching. Table~\ref{tab:timeout_index} shows why this is
inadequate: with both stages enabled, timeouts already occur at low
overheat levels below a fixed guard threshold, especially for 24MP images
and memory-pressure captures. Tightening the threshold enough to cover
those worst cases would also suppress less risky cases at the same overheat
level, such as normal 12MP captures that still complete the measured burst.
The guard therefore cannot express the real decision boundary, which varies
with workload sequence, image-size tier, memory pressure, and accumulated
queueing delay. Both errors are user-visible: a skipped effect that later
appears in the final image yields a draft--final inconsistency, and
independent per-shot decisions cause shot-to-shot jitter in continuous
capture.

\myparagraph{Simple predictors}
Replacing the guard with a straightforward latency predictor does not fare
better. A statically profiled worst-case reservation is safe but assumes a
throttled device on every capture, collapsing the admission rate exactly
when the budget is ample. A running mean or last observed value tracks the
center of the latency distribution rather than its tail, and a fixed
percentage margin cannot repair it, because the right margin is precisely
what differs across devices and thermal states. And deciding each stage in
isolation ignores the structure of the pipeline: admitting a stage because
it alone fits the budget can still starve the mandatory encoding tail
behind it.

\myparagraph{Optional and mandatory draft work}
The discussion above implies a role distinction among operations in the
draft sequence. Some operations are optional enhancements: skipping them
preserves the ability to save and deliver a draft image, but it may make the
draft visibly inconsistent with the final processed result. Other configured
operations run as part of the sequence but are not admission points in the
current system. The encoding operation is mandatory because the draft image
cannot be delivered without saving it. A timeout-prevention policy may
therefore skip optional enhancement work, but it must still leave enough
time for all non-skipped work and the mandatory save.

\myparagraph{Workloads and sequences}
We model each draft operation as a \emph{workload} $\wl \in \wset$
identified by its operation type and image-size tier, plus output format and
pending-request state for encoding. These keys let timing samples for
comparable work accumulate across captures without modeling every exact
resolution separately. Workloads play three roles: \emph{admission-gated}
workloads require a decision before execution; \emph{observed} workloads run
and are measured; and the \emph{complete} workload is the mandatory encoding
tail $\tail$. At any decision point, the remaining draft work is the ordered
suffix $\wseq = \langle \wl_1, \dots, \wl_m\rangle$. Its actual execution
time is the sum of the workload durations in the suffix. Because this
duration varies with device state and runtime interference, the framework
cannot rely on a static cost table.

\smallskip
\noindent
These observations motivate treating Capture Timeout prevention as an
\emph{admission} problem over the ordered remaining workload sequence:
before an optional stage runs, it is admitted only if a conservative upper
bound on the remaining sequence---the stage plus all work after it,
including the mandatory tail $\tail$---satisfies $\ub(\wseq) \le \bgt$.
The bound must be learned online and on-device from observed durations,
produce usable decisions from few or no samples, track drift as the device
heats and throttles, and remain consistent across sequences that share
workloads. The next section presents our context-aware admission controller
for the draft sequence, built around these requirements.
