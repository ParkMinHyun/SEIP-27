\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

Commercial mobile cameras combine multiple captured frames through
compute- and memory-intensive post-processing. In the framework studied in
this paper, post-processing was introduced together with a \emph{Draft
Sequence}: the full path collects and fuses the required frames, while the
Draft path publishes an early image and preserves a recovery result. As final
processing grew heavier, selected shot modes began postponing composition
until the camera application entered the background to protect foreground
responsiveness. This deferral leaves the Draft visible for longer. Portrait
capture was a particularly important next target: its final composition was
long running, memory intensive, and visually far from an untreated input.
Lightweight multi-frame Draft Bokeh was therefore designed to provide an
approximate early portrait and enable the heavier final composition to move to
the background.

Draft processing had already exposed a release risk. The initial Draft applied
no effect; single-frame Filter and Watermark were later added to reduce its
visible difference from the final image. On the framework's Lv0--Lv6 overheat
scale, where higher values indicate greater thermal stress, an initial
safeguard disabled optional Draft work at Lv5. A subsequent lower-tier Filter
timeout at Lv4 caused the common threshold to be lowered to Lv4, restricting
flagship devices as well. Yet this guard could not enable the new workload:
Draft Bokeh still timed out below Lv4 and consequently missed two consecutive
flagship generations.
Capture Timeout is release blocking in this product context, so an unsafe
feature must be fixed, restricted, or removed.

Parallel capture makes the problem cumulative. The hardware abstraction layer
can report readiness for another shot before the preceding Draft finishes, but
Draft Sequences execute on one worker. A new Draft can therefore consume much
of its fixed deadline while queued behind older captures. Thermal throttling,
memory pressure, scheduling, and I/O variation can lengthen one sequence and
propagate its delay through the burst. The relevant risk is thus the tail
latency of the \emph{queued and remaining sequence}, not the average latency
of an isolated image operation.

A fixed overheat boundary cannot represent that risk. Below level~4, it admits
Bokeh even when a costly sequence and accumulated backlog make the capture
unsafe. At level~4 and above, it rejects every optional stage even when the
pipeline is empty and sufficient budget remains. Lowering the threshold
removes more useful Draft processing; raising it accepts more timeout risk.
The historical guard did not cause the Bokeh failures, but its opposite errors
show that it is not a viable enablement policy for the new workload.

We present a context-aware controller with two complementary control points.
\emph{Remaining-sequence admission} omits optional work when its learned upper
bound, including the mandatory tail, does not fit the current capture's
remaining budget. This reduces demand after queueing has already consumed part
of the deadline. \emph{Capture-availability pacing} delays the next-shot
opportunity by a predicted budget deficit, allowing queued work to drain
before another deadline begins. Both decisions reuse an online,
tail-calibrated model of observed workload execution. Pacing is not free: it
can increase inter-shot latency. We therefore prioritize deadline safety and
report Draft availability and applied callback delay separately; observed
shot-to-shot timing is an end-to-end outcome.

Across three normal 12MP, 30-shot bursts---one at each of levels~4--6, where
the deployed guard would disable optional Draft work---our controller completed all 90
tested captures without a timeout and ran both Draft Bokeh and Filter on 63
captures (70.0\%). Mean applied callback delay in each burst ranged from 0.85
to 1.15 seconds, with a maximum of 1.50 seconds, quantifying the callback-level
control cost of retaining Draft functionality.

\noindent
\myparagraph{Contributions}
We make the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Industrial problem characterization.} We show how parallel
  capture and evolving Draft workloads make a fleet-wide overheat guard both
  under-protective and over-restrictive.
  \item \textbf{Coordinated runtime control.} We reuse learned tail bounds for
  current-capture workload admission and subsequent-capture pacing.
  \item \textbf{Industrial prototype evaluation.} In a commercial Android
  camera framework, we evaluate deadline safety, Draft feature availability,
  prediction quality, and pacing cost across runtime conditions.
\end{itemize}
