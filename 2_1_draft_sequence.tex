\subsection{Draft Delivery on the Capture-Critical Path}
\label{sec:draft-background}

A shutter event produces multiple image buffers. The full post-processing path
collects the required buffers, performs multi-frame composition and image
enhancement, encodes the result, and stores the final JPEG. As these operations
have become more compute- and memory-intensive, selected capture modes defer
full composition until the camera application enters the background, thereby
avoiding contention with preview rendering and preparation for the next shot.

Deferral creates a delivery gap, which the framework bridges with the Draft
Sequence. The Draft path selects its input before full composition completes,
performs a bounded amount of processing, writes a JPEG and its media-database
entry, and retains the image as a recovery result. The gallery thumbnail can
therefore expose the capture early; when full post-processing finishes, the
framework replaces the Draft with the final image. The Draft is consequently
both a user-visible intermediate result and a reliability mechanism, not an
internal preview artifact.

The growing difference between the two paths motivated richer Draft
processing. The original Draft applied no image effect, and later versions
added single-frame Filter and Watermark stages. Portrait mode raised the next
requirement. Its final result applies multi-frame processing and Bokeh, and its
full composition is sufficiently expensive to benefit from background
deferral. An untreated Draft, however, can look qualitatively different from
the final portrait. Lightweight multi-frame Draft Bokeh was developed to
provide an approximate early portrait while preserving background execution
for the heavier final composition. Figure~\ref{fig:draft-pipeline} summarizes
this intended transition.
