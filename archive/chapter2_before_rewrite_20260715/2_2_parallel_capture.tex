\subsection{Deadline Contention under Parallel Capture}
\label{sec:timeout}

Parallel capture is a modern production optimization that substantially reduces
the shot-to-shot interval. The HAL can report capture availability before all
buffers and Draft processing from the preceding shot have completed. The camera
application can therefore start the next capture while the preceding pipeline
is still running, markedly improving responsiveness during repeated shooting.

Despite this overlap, the framework serializes Draft Sequences on a single
worker to preserve the order in which results are shown and stored and to bound
their compute and memory demand while preview and ongoing capture remain active.
Parallel capture can therefore enqueue Draft work faster than the worker
completes it, causing later Drafts to wait and begin with less of their Capture
Timeout budget remaining.

Within the product line studied here, Capture Timeout compliance is one of the
highest-priority camera reliability KPIs and is enforced as a hard release gate:
a reproducible violation blocks launch until the responsible feature is fixed,
restricted, or removed. The Draft Sequence falls within this
deadline because it produces the first user-visible result, which also serves
as the recovery image. A timeout on this path is therefore treated as a capture
failure, not as a delay in optional post-processing. Let capture $i$ start at
$a_i$ and obtain the Draft worker at $s_i$. Let
$T_i^{\mathrm{effects}}$ denote the time spent on enabled effects, such as
Bokeh, Filter, and Watermark, and let $T_i^{\mathrm{tail}}$ denote the remaining
required work, including encoding and saving. Capture $i$ meets the fixed
deadline \deadline{} only if

\begin{equation}
  T_i^{\mathrm{effects}} + T_i^{\mathrm{tail}}
  \leq \underbrace{\deadline - (s_i-a_i)}_{b_i}.
  \label{eq:draft-budget}
\end{equation}

Here, $b_i$ is the budget remaining when Draft processing begins. As queueing
increases $s_i-a_i$, later captures have less time for their own Draft work.

Figure~\ref{fig:overlap} illustrates this cumulative budget loss. Even with
identical Draft execution times, later captures spend progressively more of
their budgets waiting, until the remaining budget no longer covers the
mandatory tail.

The deployed safeguard, however, considers only thermal level and observes
neither the enabled Draft workload nor the accumulated queueing delay.
