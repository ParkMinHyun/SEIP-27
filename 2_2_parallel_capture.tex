\subsection{Parallel Capture Creates Cross-Shot Interference}
\label{sec:timeout}

% FACT CHECK: confirm the 7-second product deadline before submission.
The framework raises a \emph{Capture Timeout} when the Draft result is not
delivered within the fixed 7-second deadline \deadline. Parallel capture
changes how this deadline is consumed. Once the HAL is ready to accept another
request, it can report capture availability before all buffers from the
preceding shot have arrived and before that shot's Draft Sequence has
finished. The framework can then notify the camera application, which may
start the next capture immediately. Draft Sequences, however, share a single
worker and therefore execute in order.

Figure~\ref{fig:overlap} illustrates the resulting interference. Capture $i$
starts its Draft as soon as its input is ready. Capture $i{+}1$ begins while
that Draft is still running and must wait for the same worker; capture $i{+}2$
waits behind both predecessors. Crucially, each deadline starts at its own
capture event rather than at the time its Draft obtains the worker. Queueing
therefore consumes a later capture's budget before any of its Draft stages can
run. Even with identical stage durations, the remaining budget shrinks across
the burst until a mandatory stage crosses the deadline.

  \centering

  \caption{Cross-shot deadline interference under parallel capture. Draft
  Sequences queue on one worker, but each deadline starts at the corresponding

  \label{fig:overlap}
\end{figure}

This behavior makes tail latency more important than average operation time.
A slowdown caused by thermal throttling, memory pressure, scheduling, or I/O
can lengthen one Draft Sequence and transfer its delay to every capture behind
it. The relevant unit of risk is therefore the \emph{queued and remaining
sequence}, not Bokeh or Filter measured in isolation.
