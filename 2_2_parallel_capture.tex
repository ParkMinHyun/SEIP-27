\subsection{Parallel Capture Creates Cross-Shot Interference}
\label{sec:timeout}

Parallel capture substantially shortens the shot-to-shot interval. The HAL can
report capture availability before all buffers and Draft processing from the
preceding shot have completed. The framework then notifies the camera
application, allowing the next capture to begin while the preceding pipeline is
still running. This overlap markedly improves responsiveness during repeated
shooting.

The overlap can nevertheless enqueue Draft work faster than it completes.
Although each capture uses independent processing-node instances, the production
framework schedules Draft Sequences on a single worker. Running several
high-resolution image-processing pipelines concurrently would increase peak
compute and memory contention and degrade foreground camera usability. Serial
execution bounds this contention and also preserves capture order. Parallel
captures can therefore begin in quick succession, while their Draft Sequences
execute one by one.

Meeting the Capture Timeout deadline is a mandatory pre-release KPI: the first
capture result must be produced within a fixed product deadline. The Draft
Sequence is subject to this KPI because it produces the image first shown to the
user and retained for recovery while final composition may be deferred. Its
timeout budget starts at the capture event, not when its Draft Sequence obtains
the worker. As Figure~\ref{fig:overlap} illustrates, later captures can thus
consume part of their budgets waiting behind earlier Draft work. Even with
identical execution times, the remaining budget shrinks across a burst until a
later capture incurs a \emph{Capture Timeout} during mandatory processing.

A single reproducible violation can require the affected feature to be fixed,
restricted, or removed before launch. Because queueing varies with the active
workload and burst history, the next section examines why a thermal-only
threshold cannot reliably distinguish safe from unsafe captures.
