\subsection{Limitations of Static Thermal Gating}
\label{sec:guard-limit}

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Production thermal guard.}
Samsung's overheat level is an ordinal thermal indicator on a 0--6 scale, where larger values denote stronger throttling.
During pre-release validation, level-4 throttling delayed a single-frame Draft stage and caused a Capture Timeout.
After the failure recurred despite optimization, we adopted a uniform safeguard that skips optional Draft processing at level 4 or higher.
Because the Draft Sequence is implemented in framework code shared across models, the safeguard applies uniformly rather than through model-specific branches.
However, level 4 is not confined to stress testing; it can also arise under high ambient temperatures, charging, or preceding device workloads.
Nor does the level reveal how much of a capture's deadline has already been consumed by queueing or how much Draft work remains.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Target workload and configuration.}
We denote the candidate lightweight multi-frame Draft stage by \emph{M} and the deployed all optional single-frame stages in the target Portrait configuration by \emph{S}. 
We compare M with M+S; both are followed by the same mandatory encoding and saving.
To reduce the draft--final visual gap in Section~\ref{sec:draft-background}, we aim to make M available across all supported models and levels and execute it whenever safely possible, rather than disabling it uniformly at level 4.
We first evaluated M on the Galaxy S26 Ultra, the highest-performing model, to determine whether the problem persists even on the most capable device.
With M enabled and the guard bypassed, we ran ten 30-capture trials per combination of starting level (0--6), resolution, memory condition, and Draft Sequence configuration.
The motivating level-4 failure occurred near capture 20 with 12 Draft Sequences pending, so each trial continued to 30 captures to exercise the pipeline beyond the observed failure point.
Memory-pressure tests ran the camera alongside AnTuTu Benchmark~11.1.4's memory-stress workload.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Required runtime control.}
Some trials starting at overheat levels 0--3 timed out even though the production guard permits optional Draft processing at those levels.
Conversely, at starting levels 4--6, several captures completed optional Draft processing before the earliest timeout, even though the guard would suppress it from the first capture.
Together, these results show that a fixed thermal threshold cannot reliably identify which captures have sufficient budget to execute \(M\).
The overheat level does not reflect the budget remaining for each capture: waiting behind earlier captures can consume it before the Draft worker becomes available, and throttling can further erode it after a stage begins.
Executing \(M\) for every capture would require spacing subsequent captures farther apart to prevent queued work from exhausting their budgets, directly increasing shot-to-shot latency.
Adjusting only the current capture's work is also insufficient, because it cannot recover time already spent waiting or compensate for throttling that begins after the amount of work has been fixed.
Reliably supporting multi-frame composition therefore requires two controls to act together at runtime: one that scales each capture's Draft work to its remaining budget and another that delays subsequent captures only as needed to prevent queued work from exhausting their budgets before execution begins.
