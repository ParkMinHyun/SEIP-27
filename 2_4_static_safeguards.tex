\subsection{Limitations of Fixed-Threshold Safeguards}
\label{sec:guard-limit}
\FloatBarrier

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Deployed safeguards.}
The production framework uses two fixed-threshold policies to contain Capture Timeouts without an online cost model.
First, the studied product disables optional Draft processing at and above a fixed overheat level.
Second, on the HAL-driven parallel-capture path, the framework suppresses \texttt{captureAvailable} once the number of active post-processing sequences reaches a fixed cap.
These deterministic policies impose little runtime overhead and are straightforward to validate, making them practical for incident containment.

The overheat guard nevertheless has a key limitation: its fixed boundary does not estimate whether an individual capture's remaining ordered work fits its deadline budget.
We characterized this mismatch on an anonymized flagship device that supports parallel capture.
We normalized the deployed boundary as $K$ and bypassed it to execute the optional stages across the reported levels.

Below $K$, the guard admits executions that still violate the deadline, and the failure point changes with the remaining Draft workload.
At $K-1$ under 24MP memory pressure, the first miss occurs at capture 7 with M+S and at capture 15 with M alone.
In every reported trace with an observed miss, the preceding captures meet the deadline before a later capture violates it.
Thus, the decision depends on the remaining sequence and a capture's position in the queued burst, neither of which is represented by $K$.

At and above $K$, however, the same guard suppresses optional work from the first capture even when early captures meet the deadline in the characterization.
At $K$, the normal 12MP M-only workload completes 15 captures before its first miss; at $K+2$, the corresponding 12MP and 24MP M-only workloads each complete ten captures.
These prefixes do not establish burst-wide safety; together with the below-$K$ misses, they show that overheat level alone cannot classify per-capture risk across the tested traces.
Model-specific branches would raise implementation and validation cost without resolving within-burst variation.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Limits of independent fixed gates.}
The callback gate is equally coarse: it uses the number of active post-processing sequences as a fixed proxy for outstanding capture backlog, although equal counts need not represent equal queued Draft work or equal remaining budgets.
Tightening the cap can reduce backlog in constrained contexts but can reduce shot-to-shot responsiveness on capable devices; relaxing it can preserve responsiveness but admit more backlog in slow contexts.
By the time a queued capture reaches the Draft worker, skipping optional work can also be too late for its remaining path to meet the deadline.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Implication for runtime control.}
For the richer Draft workload, these opposing errors expose a three-way trade-off among timeout compliance, optional-work retention, and shot-to-shot responsiveness.
Retuning either threshold only moves the operating point among these goals; it does not resolve the mismatch between a global proxy and per-capture deadline risk.
Fixed thresholds therefore leave two decisions unresolved: whether the current capture's remaining Draft path fits the budget left after queueing, and whether allowing the next request to start would create unsafe queueing.
