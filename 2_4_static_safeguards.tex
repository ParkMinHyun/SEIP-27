\subsection{Limitations of Static Thermal Gating}
\label{sec:guard-limit}

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Production thermal guard.}
Samsung's overheat level is an ordinal thermal indicator on a 0--6 scale, where larger values denote stronger throttling.
During pre-release validation, level-4 throttling delayed a single-frame Draft stage and caused a Capture Timeout.
After the failure recurred despite optimization, we adopted a uniform safeguard that skips optional Draft processing at level 4 or above.
Because the Draft Sequence is implemented in framework code shared across models, the safeguard applies uniformly rather than through model-specific branches.
However, level 4 is reachable in routine use under high ambient temperatures, charging, or preceding device workloads.
The level also does not reveal whether a capture's remaining budget can cover queued and remaining Draft work.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Target workload and setup.}
We denote the lightweight multi-frame Draft stage under production evaluation by \emph{M} and the deployed single-frame stage by \emph{S}.
The target Portrait Draft Sequence runs optional M and S before mandatory encoding and saving.
To reduce the draft--final visual gap in Section~\ref{sec:draft-background}, we aim to make M available across all supported models and levels and execute it whenever safely possible, rather than disabling it uniformly at level 4.
We evaluated M on the Galaxy S26 Ultra, our highest-performing model.
With M enabled and the guard bypassed, we ran ten 30-capture trials per combination of starting level (0--6), resolution, memory condition, and Draft Sequence configuration.
The level-4 failure had 12 pending sequences near capture 20; 30 captures extended beyond that state.
Memory-pressure trials ran the camera alongside AnTuTu Benchmark~11.1.4's memory-stress workload.

Some trials starting at levels 0--3 timed out despite beginning below the cutoff.
Conversely, trials starting at levels 4--6 completed several M+S captures before timing out at captures 4--10, although the guard would suppress optional work immediately.
Continuous capture can increase throttling, so the starting level is only a snapshot.
Thus, the cutoff is under-protective for some low-level starts and over-conservative for early high-level captures.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Required runtime control.}
These results call for complementary admission and pacing controls.
Admission protects the current capture by running M only when its predicted remaining work fits the live budget.
Skipping M reduces remaining work but cannot undo incurred queueing delay or absorb spikes in the remaining stages under memory pressure or increasing throttling.
Meanwhile, each new capture extends the queue, and repeated capture can intensify throttling near level transitions.
Pacing must therefore regulate subsequent captures so queued work can drain before another timeout window begins.
Together, the controls must be coordinated at runtime to preserve M whenever feasible while maintaining timeout safety.
