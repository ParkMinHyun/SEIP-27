\subsection{Limitations of Static Thermal Gating}
\label{sec:guard-limit}

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Production thermal guard.}
The Draft control logic in Samsung's camera framework is shared across device variants.
To contain the increased deadline risk under thermal throttling, Samsung's camera framework disables all Draft image-processing from Overheat Level 4, where the existing single-frame stage $S$ had caused Capture Timeouts during production validation.
Level 4 is not exceptional: it is readily reached during repeated-capture and stress-validation workloads and can also arise from high ambient temperatures, charging, and preceding device workloads.
This deterministic rule is inexpensive to validate, but it does not estimate whether the current capture can complete within its remaining deadline budget.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Target workload and setup.}
Our goal is to enable the lightweight multi-frame Draft stage M introduced in Section~\ref{sec:draft-background}.
We evaluate M alone to isolate its cost and M+S to represent its coexistence with the existing single-frame stage S.
We use an anonymized high-capability flagship whose parallel-capture path has no additional queue-length restriction, allowing us to isolate the thermal guard.
In an earlier Capture Timeout incident on a constrained flagship, the issue log showed 12 pending Draft Sequences and led to a model-specific queue-length limit on parallel capture.
Because roughly 30 consecutive portrait-mode captures are required to build a comparable backlog on the measured device at level 4, we conservatively use 30-capture bursts.
After bypassing the guard, we test overheat levels 0--6 across resolutions and normal and memory-pressure conditions.

The fixed cutoff misclassifies captures in both directions.
Below the cutoff, at level 3 under 24MP memory pressure, the first miss occurs at capture 15 with M and at capture 7 with M+S.
At the cutoff, under normal 12MP capture, M completes the first 15 captures before a miss, although the deployed rule disables it from the first capture.
The failure point also shifts with resolution, memory pressure, enabled stages, and burst position.
Lowering the cutoff suppresses more M executions that would meet the deadline, whereas raising it admits more late-burst timeouts.
Thus, the common cutoff cannot enable M across the supported operating range while preserving Capture Timeout compliance.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Required runtime control.}
These results require deciding whether to execute M for each capture based on its remaining budget and predicted Draft cost.
Yet a later capture can lose budget while waiting behind earlier Draft Sequences; the system must therefore regulate when new captures start, before the queue becomes unsafe.
Fixed queue-length limits on constrained variants address this risk by withholding \texttt{captureAvailable}, but a count does not represent the actual cost of queued work.
Supporting M across runtime conditions therefore requires a controller that coordinates per-capture admission with capture pacing from a shared runtime estimate of queued cost and remaining budget.
